Find a questionnaire and critique the design, both the format and the questions used.

Consider areas such as ‘why is this question included?’, ‘is the form of the question appropriate?’ etc. How can you improve the questionnaire?

 

I have found SANS Institute’s “Are You practicing Safe Security?” cybersecurity awareness questionnaire for consumers (SANS, 2020), which employs simple, direct questions phrased in plain language. This supports accessibility for the general public, avoiding technical jargon.

 

Using a mix of dichotomous yes/no and 5-point Likert scale questions allows capturing a range of behavioral responses beyond just binary.

 

However, the yes/no format for questions like “Do you use different passwords for different accounts?” could be enhanced by providing frequency options such as “always”, “sometimes”, “rarely” to gather more nuanced data on people’s habits. This would provide greater context around adherence levels (Boynton & Greenhalgh, 2004).

 

The security goal of each question is clear in assessing password usage, device locking, software updating, and backup practices. But the scope could be expanded to gather data on other areas like phishing and social engineering awareness, social media conduct, remote work and BYOD behaviors to enable a more holistic evaluation of cyber hygiene (Alotaibi et al., 2017).

 

Adding demographic questions on age, education, occupation, technology experience and training would allow segmenting responses based on respondent characteristics. There is a major difference between the security hygiene practised by an educated security expert, as opposed to a high school student. This would better inform targeted awareness initiatives tailored to groups with poor hygiene (Parsons et al., 2014).

 

The questionnaire length is reasonably concise at 12 core questions, supporting participation and minimising the risk of participants either ignoring the survey as it takes “too long”, or quickly finishing it without giving questions a second thought. A potential improvement is creating a question bank with rotating subsets to cover more ground while maintaining engagement.

 

The introduction could provide more motivation by citing disturbing cybercrime statistics and urging thoughtful responses. Presenting overall scoring after completion motivates reflection and next steps to improve (Kirlappos & Sasse, 2014).

 

Overall, the questionnaire effectively assesses key consumer security topics in plain language. But enhancements in contextual questions, expanded scope, demographics, scoring, and motivation elements would provide deeper insights to guide education programs aimed at better cyber hygiene.
